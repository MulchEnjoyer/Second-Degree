**I’m stuck in this new arms race**

A few decades ago, the nuclear arms race worked like this: no country necessarily wanted to build nuclear weapons, but once one country did, every other country had to follow. Not because they liked the idea, but because the cost of falling behind was too high.

Something similar is starting to happen in academia.

Except this new weapon isn’t nuclear. It’s productivity.

**Effort and Assessment**

For most of modern academic history, the unit of effort was relatively stable. A student might read a set of papers, think about them, draft an essay, revise it, and submit something that reflected a few days or weeks of intellectual work.

The friction was part of the system.

Reading slowly.  
Synthesising ideas.  
Struggling through drafts.

That friction wasn’t accidental. It was where most of the thinking actually happened. But generative AI has begun to dissolve that friction and universities are still trying to figure out what that means.

The University of Sydney recently introduced what it calls a **“two-lane” approach to assessment**. One lane consists of secure, supervised assessments where AI tools are restricted. The other lane includes open assignments where AI use is allowed and sometimes integrated into the learning process.

On paper, this seems like a reasonable compromise. Exams still test individual knowledge. Essays and projects reflect the reality that students now work in a world where AI tools exist.

But the lived experience inside classrooms feels less like a carefully designed system and more like everyone improvising at the same time.

Students are experimenting.  
Professors are experimenting.  
Universities are experimenting.

No one seems entirely sure what the end state is supposed to look like.

**The fun police is here**

In one assignment I encountered last week in my legal ethics class, students were **forced and required to include Microsoft Copilot somewhere in their workflow.**

Not necessarily to write the essay.  
Not necessarily to generate research.

Just… somewhere.

There were no strict guidelines. No step-by-step explanation of what counted as meaningful use.

Only the requirement that it be used.

Which raises a strange question.

If a university requires AI in the workflow but cannot clearly define what that workflow should be, what exactly is being assessed?

- The assignment?  
- The process?  
- Or simply the willingness to participate in an institutional experiment?

But there is an even stranger element here.

This wasn’t simply a case of AI being allowed.

It was **required**.

For most of the past two years, the conversation in universities has revolved around whether generative AI should be banned or permitted. The assumption was that the core problem was academic integrity.

- Can students use it?  
- Should they use it?  
- How do you detect it?

But once an assignment explicitly requires AI as part of the workflow, the question quietly shifts.

It is no longer about permission.

Students are not simply allowed to use AI.

They are expected to.

Which makes the moment feel less like a policy and more like an experiment unfolding in real time.

**Volume and Equity**

The real shift, however, isn’t about AI tools themselves but the **expectations of outputs**.

A student who previously produced a 2,000-word essay might now produce far more. Research summaries appear faster. Drafts multiply. Iteration becomes cheaper.

This doesn’t necessarily mean students are finishing work faster.

Instead, they’re doing **more work within the same timeframe.**

Which sounds productive, until you realise what happens next.

Professors read the submissions.  
The average volume rises.  
The average polish rises.

And the implicit baseline shifts.

Suddenly, what used to be normal effort starts to look thin, and a new equilibrium quietly forms.

**The productivity arms race**

This is where the arms race analogy becomes useful.

No single student decides that everyone should work three times harder.

But if some students use AI to produce dramatically more output, others must follow just to remain competitive.

The result is a classic coordination problem.

If everyone slowed down together, the system might stabilise.

But no individual student can afford to opt out.

The cost of doing so is falling behind.

The productivity arms race does not require a central planner.

It emerges naturally.

**The amplification question**

There is another question hiding beneath all of this.

In venture capital circles, Harry Stebbings often asks founders a simple question about AI:

> Does AI make every engineer ten times better?  
> Or does it make the best engineers a hundred times better?

The same question applies to students.

Does AI lift everyone equally?

Or does it amplify the students who already know how to think, structure arguments, and conduct research?

A skilled student might use AI as a cognitive accelerator—testing ideas, generating counterarguments, or refining structure.

Another student might simply generate text and submit it.

Both technically used AI.

But the outcomes could be radically different.

If the second scenario is closer to reality, AI may not level the academic playing field.

It may widen it.

**Work and university are merging**

There is also a broader cultural shift happening around university itself.

For many students today, academia is no longer a sealed intellectual environment.

Students juggle:

- part-time work  
- internships  
- side projects  
- startups  
- coursework

The rhythms of the workplace increasingly bleed into academic life.

And the workplace has already embraced productivity tools.

Slack.  
Notion.  
Automation.  
AI.

In that environment, using AI in university assignments feels less like cheating and more like applying the same workflow used everywhere else.

But this shift carries a subtle consequence.

Instead of freeing time, AI often compresses **more work into the same number of hours.**

You don’t necessarily finish earlier.

You just produce more.

**Where now**

The strangest part of this moment is that nobody fully understands where it leads.

Universities are redesigning assessments in real time because detecting AI-generated work reliably is nearly impossible.

Students are experimenting with new workflows.

Policies are evolving semester by semester.

And the system keeps moving forward anyway.

Perhaps this is simply what technological transitions look like from the inside:

Messy.  
Improvised.  
Slightly disorienting.

The printing press did something similar to scholarship centuries ago.

The internet did it again.

Now generative AI is doing it to the process of thinking itself.

The real question may not be whether AI belongs in academia.

That debate may already be over.

The more interesting question is whether **productivity itself has become impossible to opt out of.**

And if it has, what happens to learning when the friction that once made thinking slow—and difficult—is no longer required.
