**Silence**

Over the past four weeks I’ve had the amazing opportunity to compete in five competitions. Five series of sleepless nights. I’ve also had five opportunities to reflect during the silence immediately following the team meetings. It’s hard to fight the feeling of knowing the incumbent trudge that awaits the moment the minutes hit your inbox.

That silence is no stranger.

I used to tell myself during that same moment of silence following a brief during my military training that the incoming journey would be for a well fought reward. There was always a reward. My personal vice was chasing my mother with my sweat filled uniform upon reaching home. I remember the whiffs taken before throwing the clothes in the washer. It wasn’t just the smell either—the crystals that formed along the back of the shirt, the brown crust of mud that would form on your bum. In hindsight they were all peacocking the signals of hard work done.

The proof of hard work was in the pudding, if the metaphorical pudding was a polyester sludge within a ziplock bag.

It was one of the first times in adulthood that I could feel proud of the work I had done with no tie to achievement. I find it fascinating that the Army and Airforce were the only places in my life where I observed multiple people completing the exact same task while still individually feeling a form of pride.

You could argue that the patches themselves were a form of trophying, something to be earned. But personally, and I’d like to think for others as well, it was always about the stories.

Now why NS story syndrome exists among guys is another thing, but partially I think it’s the mutual understanding amongst the guys. It’s a universal language not just all Singaporean males share, but military alumni alike understand and respect. I’ve found myself talking and relating to regulars from an Australian ADF infantry unit I met in Hanoi to a British Airforce pilot in Rome.

There was always a familiarity there.

I’m privileged enough to say that there is an endless pool of competitions for me.


**Love 4 da game**

Over the past year I’ve had the opportunity to qualify, compete, and both win and lose with the help of amazing teammates. I’ve podiumed a few, lost a lot more, but ultimately always felt like there was something to gain.

Competitions are built on the premise that participants receive value. Some chase the CV line. Others recognise the developmental growth. I’d like to say I initially participated for development, but I’d be lying. Six months before even joining university I already knew the exact clubs and competition types I wanted to win.

I remember talking to someone two years senior to me before starting in Singapore about the infamous CV checklist. Podium one or two competitions, then exit. Forget mentoring the next generation of students.

I quickly realised, after inevitably losing my first two competitions, that I loved the process of problem solving. But secretly I developed a different vice.

I loved pitching.

I loved that you could ransack a business process or idea, tearing it apart while building a solution. The judge would stare at your presentation with a smile while you essentially told them their assumptions were wrong.

I felt this weird invincibility while competing. It was different from academia or sports. I could lie through my teeth about business processes, estimates, evaluation methods, demo features—anything to convince the judge that their assumptions were wrong and that I was fucking brilliant.

And the crazy thing was, there were no consequences.

Part of my addiction to competing came from that flexibility. You could perform brilliance without needing to prove it afterwards.


**The Cracks Start Appearing**

I distinctly remember two moments that changed how I saw competitions.

**Moment One: Hackathons Are Just Better**

I was reflecting on the hackathon experience I had during the ElevenLabs Global Hackathon in Singapore with a good friend of mine. During that event I met some of the coolest people in industry and they built some of the coolest shit I had ever seen—in four hours.

Before that event I didn’t even think you could scope a real problem in four hours.

The prizes were also insane. High four figures, sometimes even five. I could have never imagined prizes like that for case competitions.

What struck me wasn’t just the money. It was the community. People were actually building things. Industry people were hanging around. The effort translated directly into something tangible.

Comparatively, the school competitions I was doing felt like they were missing both community and compensation.

Maybe I’m wrong. I’ve never competed internationally representing the school in finance or consulting competitions. But at that point I was still chasing podiums, and CV placement would have been enough.

Still, that moment stuck with me.

The idea that effort could be converted directly into something real.


**Moment Two: Misrepresentation**

The second moment was the Marshall Case Competition in 2026.

I saw what looked like an amazing result—our school placing second in the short case competition. But after digging further I realised they had placed second in their seed. Out of five teams.

Now I’m not diminishing their achievement. Placing second in any competition is still an achievement. But the structure of the competition was absurd.

Marshall formatted the competition so that there were four seeds, each with three podium positions, across three competitions. Each seed had at most five competing teams. This meant every team had roughly a 2/3 chance of podiuming just by participating.

Practices like that inflate prestige while quietly diluting the meaning of the achievement. In my opinion they should be brought to light and ridiculed.

And this isn’t just one competition.

The dilution of achievement has been exacerbated by the sheer number of competitions available at any one time. Clubs, schools, and companies are all trying to build communities through distribution.

But what happens when everyone is giving out podiums?

What is the value of fighting for another line on the CV when others have plenty? Is industry even aligned with the reality of how many opportunities exist now?

Especially in ANZ, where the internship pipeline connected to competitions is frankly terrible compared to other regions.

The value proposition just started feeling off.


**Apparently There Are No Consequences, Yet I’m Burned Out**

I’m physically drained. Pulling investment banking hours for zero reward has only heightened my angst. My addiction to pitching combined with the dilution of reward-to-effort has left a distaste for problem solving in the competition realm.

I’m out of love.

When opportunities to feed the addiction are plentiful and the cost of participation is low, are there really any consequences to attempting things half-heartedly? Does it even make sense to put in effort?

This value misalignment has only heightened my anger toward teammates who are slower or doubtful over deliverables, and guilt toward my own participation.

But what if I’m right?

What if it truly doesn’t matter?

I’m currently on a team aligned around the goal of podiuming. But does it actually matter? Why put everything into one competition when you could do five? Or ten?

It’s not like the well is going to dry up anytime soon.

Hard work without results means nothing in the real world. Results have to arise somehow, even if that’s over the long term.

But competitions compress everything into these tiny bursts of artificial urgency.

And afterwards?

Nothing.

Just the next competition.


**The Prize for the Pie Eating Contest**

The temporariness has left me feeling empty.

Sure, I get great interview stories and a ridiculous amount of domain knowledge. But following a win is just the pathway to another win.

The prize for the pie eating contest is more pie.

I used to think I enjoyed eating pie. Maybe I did. But pie eating isn’t for me anymore.


**No More Pie — So What?**

I’m still lost on what to do.

I’ve spent so much time eating pie that pie eating is all I know.

Maybe I should become a baker. Maybe I should just eat pie alone. Maybe I should join the apple bobbing contest instead. Or maybe I should just become a decathlete and try everything.

All I know is that after the pie eating contest this weekend, I’m done.

Or maybe I won’t be.

Maybe I’ll relapse.
